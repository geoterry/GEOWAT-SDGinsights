Inequalities in water and sanitation access

Tracking inequalities in water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) services is essential for achieving universal access for all. When SDG monitoring is calling for methods and data sources for measuring inequalities in access to WASH services, DHS modelled surfaces provide a potential to reveal inequalities in services within countries. The inherent spatial nature of DHS modelled surfaces enable indicators to be disaggregated by specific geographic locations, such as subnational administrative areal units, urban and rural areas. By combining with supplementary geospatial data, indicators can be further disaggregated by other characteristics such as wealth.

CASE STUDY 2: Revealing inequalities in water and sanitation access by wealth and subnational regions in Tanzania

One of the most pronounced inequalities in access to drinking water and sanitation relates to location, typically measured in terms of urban and rural areas. Recent JMP study also found large disparities in WASH services between sub-national regions. Spatially interpolated data concerning drinking water and sanitation access such as the DHS modelled surfaces therefore ... as they inherently allow data to be spatially disaggregated. By combining with subnational administrative boundaries, it is possible to reveal the difference in coverage between subnational areas and therefore provide a useful comparative measure of inequality in service.
In addition, such spatially disaggregated data could also provide a potential to integragate WASH-related indicators with other geospatial data by simply matching the geographic locations. In this case study, we derived 1km poverty data from the Worldpop dataset to overlay with the DHS drinking-water and sanitation surfaces; areas where over 50% of residents per grid square living in poverty defined by the Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) are considered as 'poor areas', whilst below 50% of residents per grid square living in MPI-defined poverty are considered as 'rich areas'.
