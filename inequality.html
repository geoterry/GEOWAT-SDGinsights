Inequalities in water and sanitation access

When SDG monitoring is calling for methods and data sources for measuring inequalities in water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) access, DHS modelled surfaces could provide a potential to reveal inequalities in services within countries. The inherent spatial nature of DHS modelled surfaces enable indicators to be disaggregated by specific geographic locations, such as subnational administrative areal units, urban and rural areas. By combining with supplementary geospatial data, indicators can be further disaggregated by other characteristics such as wealth.

Example 1: Revealing inequalities in water and sanitation access by wealth


Method:


The DHS modelled surfaces allow indicators concerning drinking-water and sanitation access to be disaggregated geospatially, which therefore provides a potential to integragate WASH-related indicators with other geospatial data by matching the geographic locations. In this example, we derived 1km poverty data from the Worldpop dataset to overlay with the DHS drinking-water and sanitation surfaces; areas where over 50% of residents per grid square living in poverty defined by the Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) are considered as 'poor areas', whilst below 50% of residents per grid square living in MPI-defined poverty are considered as 'rich areas'.
